Pilot surfaces

What a team pilot is meant to include

  • Team invitation flow and pilot onboarding
  • Weekly team recovery digest
  • Shared reset recommendations for screen-heavy roles
  • Manager-facing usage summary and adoption trends
Later surfaces

What should come after the pilot is working

  • Slack or calendar-aware recovery prompts
  • Policy controls for work hours and quiet hours
  • Team-level recovery mix and timing patterns
  • Deeper reporting or admin exports if real demand appears

How the first pilot should run

Keep it tight. Sell a specific recovery program to a real team, not a broad enterprise promise.

1
Pick the team and the symptom mix

Start with remote or hybrid teams where screen-heavy work, meetings, and long focus blocks are the normal day.

2
Run a 2-week or 4-week pilot

Measure adoption, resets per person, active days, and whether the weekly recovery story starts stabilizing.

3
Review the rhythm, then expand or stop

If the team is actually using it, expand. If not, learn fast and avoid fake “enterprise readiness” theater.

Questions a serious buyer will ask

Answer them directly. This page should help filter for teams that want a practical pilot, not vague wellness storytelling.

Is the team layer fully live today?

No. The product is in pilot-first positioning. The consumer app and recovery system are real; the team admin and rollout layer are being shaped honestly around pilot demand.

Who should buy first?

Remote-first startups, agencies, product teams, engineering orgs, and support-heavy teams with long screen sessions are the clearest early buyers.

What proves the pilot worked?

Active days, resets per person, repeat usage after week one, and a recovery story that becomes more stable instead of more sporadic.

Why not sell per seat immediately?

Because a flat pilot price is easier to buy early, easier to deliver honestly, and better aligned with a product that is still learning its B2B shape.
